# Product

## Register

product

## Users

RevOps managers, GTM engineers, BDRs, and revenue architects at B2B companies using HubSpot or Salesforce. They are on this site to run agents — not to evaluate whether the concept is interesting. They work at a desk, during business hours, on a laptop or monitor. They already understand what CRM hygiene problems look like; they don't need to be sold on why it matters.

## Product Purpose

RevTown is an open-source catalog of AI agents that audit CRM data and automate RevOps tasks. Each agent ("citizen") owns a specific domain — data quality, pipeline health, BDR follow-up, forecast accuracy — and produces a structured report of what's broken and how to fix it. The interaction model mirrors Docker Hub: browse agents, pull one, run it against your CRM. The catalog is the product.

## Brand Personality

Sharp, clean, practitioner-built. The French town metaphor ("residents," "citizens," "Le Directeur") is a wink, not a theme park. Voice is confident and direct — written by someone who has run the GTM motion, not explaining it from the outside.

## Anti-references

- Generic AI product landing pages (Jasper, Copy.ai template sites) — "we help teams move faster" filler
- Sloppy or mock designs — anything that looks like a Figma prototype that was never refined
- LLM-default aesthetics — gradient blobs, glass cards, hero metrics, "10x your pipeline" copy
- Over-branded dev tools that mistake personality for decoration

## Design Principles

1. **Pull, don't pitch** — every agent should feel immediately runnable. The action is always one click away. No funnel, no gate.
2. **Practitioner first** — copy and structure read like they were written by someone who has filed a sales ops ticket, not someone describing what one is.
3. **Earned simplicity** — sparse from precision, not from omission. Nothing missing, nothing decorative for its own sake.
4. **The catalog is the interface** — the Agent Hub is the product. The homepage is the door.
5. **Intentional over decorative** — every visual decision earns its place. If it can be removed without loss, remove it.
